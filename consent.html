The Effects of Performance Judgements on Decision Making
Participant information sheet
updated January 2019

Welcome. Below you will find information that will help you to decide whether to take part in this study.

General information. This study investigates the effects of performance judgements such as confidence on how people make subsequent decisions and learn. By further understanding the processes underlying decision making and learning we aim to add to the growing knowledge of human behaviour and cognition.

We appreciate your interest in participating in this online survey. You have been invited to take part in this research because you have indicated that you might be interested in participating, and you are currently (i) aged 18-40, (ii) fluent English speaking, (iii) have normal or corrected-to- normal vision, (iv) neurologically healthy, and (v) not on any medication with psychoactive effects. Please read through these terms before agreeing to participate by ticking the ‘yes’ box below. You may ask any questions before taking part by contacting the researcher (contact us).

We (the Attention and Cognitive Control Lab at the Department of Experimental Psychology, University of Oxford) are investigating how performance judgements (e.g. confidence levels) are used in the process of decision making. A computer-based task involving simple discrimination or categorization judgments will be used. During the computer-based experiment, you will be presented with visual and/or auditory stimuli, and will then be asked to make judgments about them or about your performance in the task, as per the instructions given before the task. You may also be asked to answer some questions relevant to the task. The study will take approximately 1 hour.

Do I have to take part? Please note that your participation is voluntary. You may withdraw at any point during the questionnaire for any reason, before submitting your answers, by pressing the ‘Exit’ button / closing the browser. However, we are only able to reimburse participants who complete the full survey.

How will your data be used? We collect data on your performance during the study. We will make no attempt to identify you, or link your performance to any personal data we hold. Your data will be stored in a password-protected file and may be used in academic publications. Your IP address will not be stored.

Who will have access to your data? Prolific is the data controller with respect to your personal data and, as such, will determine how your personal data is used. Please see their privacy notice here . Prolific will share only fully anonymised data with the University of Oxford, for the purposes of research.

We would like your permission to use your anonymised data in future studies, and to share data with other researchers (e.g. in online databases).
This study is for a DPhil project. The principal researcher is Naomi Carlebach, who is attached to the Department of Experimental Psychology at the University of Oxford. This project is being completed under the supervision of Professor Nick Yeung.
This project has been reviewed by, and received ethics clearance through, the University of Oxford Central University Research Ethics Committee [reference: R61894/RE001].

What if there is a problem? If you have a concern about any aspect of this project, please speak to the relevant researcher Naomi Carlebach [naomi.carlebach@psy.ox.ac.uk]) or her supervisor Prof Nick Yeung [nicholas.yeung@psy.ox.ac.uk], who will do their best to answer your query. The researchers should acknowledge your concern within 10 working days and give you an indication of how they intend to deal with it. If you remain unhappy or wish to make a formal complaint, please contact the chair of the Research Ethics Committee at the University of Oxford (Chair, Medical Sciences Inter-Divisional Research Ethics Committee; Email: ethics@medsci.ox.ac.uk; Address: Research Services, University of Oxford, Wellington Square, Oxford OX1 2JD). The chair will seek to resolve the matter in a reasonably expeditious manner.
